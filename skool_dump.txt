Work through them roughly in order. The early prompts scaffold the engine; the later ones add the validation layers

Prompt 1 — The Backtester Scaffold
Build the core engine everything else plugs into.
AI Pathways · 2026 Track B · B6
C L A U D E C O D E P R O M P T
Build a backtesting engine in Python for a trading strategy, structured as a clean,
separate module I can extend. Create this structure:
backtest/
 engine.py # the core event-driven backtest loop
 data_handler.py # loads and serves historical OHLCV data, point-in-time
 strategy_base.py # abstract base class strategies inherit from
 portfolio.py # tracks positions, cash, and equity over time
 metrics.py # computes performance metrics from the equity curve
 costs.py # models commissions, spread, and slippage
 results.py # stores and summarizes a backtest run
Requirements:
- The engine must be strictly point-in-time: at each step, a strategy can only see
data up to and including the current bar, never future bars. Enforce this in
data_handler so look-ahead is structurally impossible.
- data_handler serves bars one at a time in chronological order.
- portfolio tracks the full equity curve, not just the final value.
- Every trade records entry time, exit time, size, entry price, exit price, and
PnL.
- Include a simple example strategy (a moving-average crossover) so I can run the
engine end to end immediately.
- Write unit tests for the engine, and a test that fails if any strategy tries to
access a future bar.
Explain the design as you build it, and keep the components separate so I can swap
strategies and add validation layers later.
Prompt 2 — The Look-Ahead Test
The single most important safety check. Build it early and run it on everything.
AI Pathways · 2026 Track B · B6
C L A U D E C O D E P R O M P T
Add a look-ahead bias detector to my backtesting engine. This is the most important
validation tool, so make it strict.
Build backtest/look_ahead_test.py that:
- Runs the strategy normally and records every decision and the data available at
that moment.
- Verifies that no feature, indicator, or signal at time T uses any data from after
time T. This includes checking that rolling calculations, normalization, and
scaling use only past data.
- Specifically tests for the classic mistakes: using the current bar's close to
make a decision that would execute at the current bar's open, and computing any
statistic (mean, std, min, max) over a window that includes future data.
- Includes a deliberately broken strategy with look-ahead bias, and confirms the
test catches it.
- Fails loudly with a clear message identifying exactly where the leak is.
Make this runnable as part of my test suite so I can run it on any strategy before
trusting its backtest. Explain what each check is catching.

The Kelly Criterion

AI Pathways 2026 Track B · Foundation
T H E F A M O U S F O R M U L A
What Kelly Says
A mathematical answer to how much to bet, given your edge and your odds.
The Kelly Criterion calculates the bet size that maximizes long-run growth, based on your edge and the
payoff odds. Bigger edge, bigger bet. It's elegant and it's the theoretical optimum. But it assumes you
know your true win rate and payoff exactly, which in trading you never do, and that gap is why full
Kelly is dangerous in practice.
Kelly is the theoretical best, under assumptions that don't quite hold in real trading.
AI Pathways 2026 Track B · Foundation
W H Y Y O U C U T I T D O W N
Fractional Kelly
Use a fraction of what Kelly suggests. You capture most of the growth with far less risk.
Full Kelly's problem
• Wildly volatile in practice
• Assumes you know your true edge exactly
• One bad estimate and it oversizes badly
• Drawdowns that few can stomach
Fractional Kelly (half, quarter)
• Far smoother equity curve
• Forgiving of estimate errors
• Captures most of the long-run growth
• Survival over theoretical optimality
Half or quarter Kelly is the practical choice. Full Kelly is a fast way to a brutal drawdown.

AI Pathways 2026 Market-Neutral Cheat Code (Statistical Arbitrage)

99% of retail traders only know how to make money if the market goes in the direction they guessed. If they buy a stock and the whole market crashes, they lose money.

Quants build "Market Neutral" strategies. The most famous is Statistical Arbitrage (Pairs Trading).

Instead of betting on one stock, we find two stocks that are historically glued together—like Visa (V) and Mastercard (MA), or Coca-Cola (KO) and Pepsi (PEP). Because they are in the exact same sector with the exact same macro headwinds, their price action is highly correlated.

But sometimes, due to random market noise or an institutional block trade, their prices temporarily snap apart. One goes up, the other goes down.

Quants view this like a stretched rubber band. They know the math dictates these two stocks must eventually snap back together. So, they Short the one that went up, and Buy the one that went down. * If the whole market crashes, the short position makes money, covering the long.

If the whole market rockets up, the long position makes money, covering the short. You are completely insulated from market direction. You only profit from the spread closing.

To do this, we don't just look at a chart. We use a heavy statistical test called Co-integration and calculate the Z-Score of their price spread.

that a retrained model is a new hypothesis that must re-earn its place, not an automatic continuation. Tell me honestly
what the warning signs are that I should just retire this edge.
What good output looks like: Monitoring for performance decay, feature drift, and regime context against validated
expectations, plus a retraining discipline that requires full re-validation and includes kill criteria. It should frame
retraining as re-earning, not continuation.
Red flags that it failed: It sets up retraining on a blind schedule with no re-validation, or no kill criteria. Send back: "Add
the requirement that every retrain re-validates with purged CV and deflated Sharpe, and give me explicit criteria for

PART 1 — THE MINDSET: EDGE DISCOVERY IS ADVERSARIAL
1. What "edge discovery" actually is (and isn't)
The name oversells it. You are not discovering a money printer. You are searching a large space of hypotheses for
something that might have a small, fragile, decaying statistical edge, and then trying as hard as you possibly can to
prove that edge is fake before you risk a dollar on it.
That reframe is the entire module. An edge is something you try to kill, not something you fall in love with. Every step
that follows, feature engineering, model selection, validation, serves one question: is this real, or is it noise? If you take
nothing else from this module: the default assumption for any discovered edge is "it's noise until proven otherwise,"
and the burden of proof is high.
The reason this matters so much in ML specifically: a simple rule-based strategy has limited degrees of freedom, so
there's only so much it can overfit. An ML model with hundreds of features and flexible structure has enormous
degrees of freedom. It can memorize the past in ways that look exactly like learning. The flexibility that makes ML
powerful is the same flexibility that makes it lie.
2. The quant cousin of sycophancy
If you've gone through the web app module, you know Claude will tend to agree with you if you let it, and that you
have to actively make it argue against you. ML has the exact same failure mode in a different costume: the model
agrees with your data.
You bring a dataset and a hope. The model's job, as far as its optimizer is concerned, is to fit that data as well as
possible. It has no concept of "this pattern is spurious" or "I wouldn't have known this in real time." It will happily
confirm whatever structure exists in your sample, including structure that exists purely by chance. Your job is to be the
adversary the optimizer isn't, to assume every result is a false positive and design tests specifically to expose it.
So the same discipline carries over: don't ask "did it work?" Ask "what would prove this is fake, and have I run that
test?"

3. Why most retail ML trading fails
It's worth being concrete about the common failure paths, because you'll recognize yourself heading down them:
Trying to predict price directly. The most common beginner move and the fastest path to ruin. Price is close to a
random walk at short horizons; predicting its direction reliably is nearly impossible, and a model that appears to do
it is almost certainly leaking or overfitting.
Feature leakage. Accidentally feeding the model information that wouldn't have existed at decision time. Easy to
do, and it produces strong-looking results that are entirely fake.
The multiple-comparisons trap. Testing hundreds of features and thousands of combinations, then getting excited
about the ones that look great, when some were guaranteed to look great by chance alone.
Backtest overfitting. Tuning the model and strategy until the historical curve looks great, with no real out-of-sample
test, so you've just memorized the past.
Ignoring decay. Treating a discovered edge as permanent when edges erode as the market adapts, often within
months.
Underestimating costs. A signal with a tiny edge gets eaten alive by spreads, slippage, and commissions the
backtest ignored.
Every one of these is preventable. Most of this module is the prevention.
PART 2 — WHAT ML IS ACTUALLY GOOD FOR
4. The capability map
ML in trading is good at some things and terrible at others. Knowing the difference is most of the battle.
What it's useful for: - Ranking and classification. Of these 50 setups that fired today, which are most likely to work? Is
this signal strong or weak? These are problems ML handles well because you're sorting and scoring, not forecasting a
precise future value. - Regime identification. What state is the market in right now, and how should that change my
behavior? This is your HMM and related hidden-state models, the layer you already know. - Conditional probability
estimation. Given these conditions, what's the historical probability this setup reaches its target before its stop? Useful
for sizing and filtering, not for magic. - Feature compression and structure-finding. Reducing many correlated
indicators to a few meaningful dimensions, or clustering setups into types you didn't know existed.
What it's bad at, and where people die: - Direct price prediction. "What will SPY close at tomorrow?" is close to
unanswerable. Models that appear to do it are leaking or overfitting almost every time. - Anything at very short
horizons without infrastructure. Sub-second and intraday microstructure edges exist but require speed and data retail
doesn't have. You're competing with firms that see the order book before you do. - Regimes it has never seen. A model
trained only on a bull market knows nothing about a crash. ML interpolates within its training distribution and fails
outside it, which is exactly when you most need it to work.
The practical rule: frame your problem as ranking, classifying, or estimating a conditional probability over a horizon
long enough that you can actually act, never as predicting price.

5. Features are where the edge lives
The model is rarely the hard part. Two competent practitioners with the same features and different model types will
get similar results; two with the same model and different features will get wildly different ones. The edge, if it exists,
lives in the features, not the algorithm. This is the single most important practical insight in the module, and it's where
Claude Code earns its keep, helping you build, transform, and reason about inputs.
A feature is just a number you compute from available information that you think might carry predictive signal. The
craft is in making them clean and informative.
Stationarity. Most ML assumes the statistical properties of your inputs are roughly stable over time. Raw price is not
stationary (it trends, and a model trained at $100 is lost at $400). Returns, ratios, and normalized indicators are far
more stationary. Transform raw levels into things that mean the same thing across time and price regimes.
Normalization and scaling. Features on wildly different scales distort many models. Z-scoring (subtract mean, divide by
standard deviation), rank-transforming, or min-max scaling puts them on comparable footing. Critically, you must
compute the scaling parameters on training data only and apply them forward, never on the full dataset, or you've
leaked the future (more on this in validation).
Predictive vs coincident features. A coincident feature tells you what's happening now; a predictive one tells you
what's likely next. Many popular indicators are coincident dressed up as predictive. A feature that's just a smoothed
version of the thing you're trying to predict carries no real edge. Ask of every feature: would knowing this before the
move have actually helped, or does it only look useful in hindsight?
Feature decay. A feature that carried edge in 2019 may be dead now because the market adapted or the regime
changed. Features have shelf lives. Part of validation is checking whether a feature's usefulness is stable across time or
concentrated in one period.
The leakage minefield. This is so important it gets its own section in validation, but flag it now: the most common way
to build a feature that costs you is to include information from the future. Using the day's close to compute a feature
for a decision you'd make at the day's open. Normalizing using statistics computed over the whole dataset including
future data. Labeling with information that wouldn't be known yet. Each one produces strong, fake results.
6. Labeling: the quietly critical choice
How you define what the model is predicting (the label, or target) is as important as the features and far more
overlooked. If you label naively, you teach the model the wrong thing.
The naive approach is the fixed-horizon label: did the price go up over the next N bars? The problem is it ignores the
path. A trade that drifts up 0.1% over five days and one that spikes 5% then craters both get labeled "up," which is
nonsense for a real trader who has a stop and a target.
The better approach, the triple-barrier method, labels based on what actually happens first: does price hit your profit
target, your stop loss, or run out of time? This labels the outcome the way you'd actually experience it as a trader, and
it's the standard in serious retail quant work. Claude Code can build this labeling for you, and the build library includes
a prompt for it.
The meta-point: your label should reflect the real trading decision and its real risk management, not a sterile "did it go
up." Get the label wrong and everything downstream is learning to solve the wrong problem perfectly.

PART 3 — THE MODEL SURVEY
This is a survey of the model families worth knowing, with a practical take on where each fits in retail trading. A
recurring theme: simpler, more interpretable models are easier to validate and harder to overfit, so they're the right
default for most problems. That doesn't mean the more advanced families have no place; it means they ask more of
you in data and validation discipline to use well. HMMs for regime appear here as the layer you build on, not something
to re-learn, since the earlier material covers them.
7. Tree ensembles (your workhorse)
Gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost) and random forests are the dominant tool in serious retail
quant for a reason. They handle tabular signal data beautifully, capture non-linear relationships and interactions
between features without you specifying them, are relatively robust, and come with built-in feature-importance
measures that help you interpret what's happening.
Random forests train many decorrelated trees on bootstrapped samples and average them. They're hard to overfit
badly, forgiving of hyperparameters, and a solid baseline. Slightly less sharp than boosting but more robust.
Gradient boosting builds trees sequentially, each correcting the last's errors. More powerful and usually higherperforming, but easier to overfit and more sensitive to tuning. LightGBM is fast and the common default; XGBoost is
the well-documented standard; CatBoost handles categorical features gracefully.
For most retail edge discovery on tabular features, a gradient-boosted tree model is where you start and often where
you end. If you can't find an edge with boosted trees on good features, a fancier model almost certainly won't save you,
it'll just overfit more convincingly.
8. Regularized linear models (the baseline)
Lasso and Ridge regression are linear models with a penalty that shrinks coefficients toward zero, fighting overfitting
and, in Lasso's case, performing automatic feature selection by zeroing out useless inputs.
They matter for two reasons. First, they're a reality check: if a simple regularized linear model finds no edge in your
features, be very skeptical of a complex model that claims to. Second, the world is noisier and more linear than
beginners think, and a well-regularized linear model is often competitive with fancier approaches while being far more
interpretable and far less prone to overfitting. Always run one as a baseline. If your gradient-boosted model can't beat a
regularized linear model out of sample, the complexity is buying you overfitting, not edge.
9. Hidden-state and regime models (the layer you know)
You've covered HMMs already, so briefly: these identify unobserved market states (calm trend, choppy range, high-vol
stress) and let your system switch behavior to match. In this module, regime detection is the context layer you build on
top of. A signal model that ignores regime is fragile; the same signal can be predictive in one regime and noise in
another.
The advanced move beyond plain HMMs: regime-conditional modeling. Instead of one model for all conditions, you
train or weight models per regime, or you feed the regime state in as a feature so the model can learn regimeAI Pathways · 2026 Page 4
dependent behavior. There are also richer state models (regime-switching models with more structure, and changepoint detection methods) worth knowing exist, but the practical win for most people is simply: always condition on
regime, never ignore it.
10. Unsupervised methods (discovery, not prediction)
Clustering (k-means, hierarchical, DBSCAN) and dimensionality reduction (PCA, and for visualization, t-SNE/UMAP)
don't predict anything directly. They find structure.
Their real use in edge discovery is twofold. Setup discovery: cluster historical market conditions or setups and you may
find natural groupings, types of days or environments you didn't know to look for, which then become features or
filters. Feature compression: when you have many correlated indicators, PCA can reduce them to a few orthogonal
dimensions, cutting noise and multicollinearity. Use these to understand your data and generate hypotheses, not to
trade directly. A cluster is a question ("is this type of environment special?"), not an answer.
11. The advanced frontier (deep learning, sequence models, RL)
Beyond trees and linear models sits a more advanced frontier: deep learning, sequence models like LSTMs, and
reinforcement learning. These are more powerful and more flexible, and that flexibility cuts both ways: it lets them
capture structure simpler models miss, and it gives them more room to overfit, which raises the bar on the data and
validation discipline needed to use them well. They reward practitioners who bring enough data and rigorous
validation, and they punish those who don't. Here's the shape of each; the dedicated Deep Learning, Sequence Models
& RL module covers them in depth with build prompts.
Deep learning (neural networks). Excels at problems with large datasets and complex, high-dimensional structure. In
trading, the natural fits are rich or alternative data (text, order-book depth, cross-sectional panels) rather than a
handful of tabular indicators, where a gradient-boosted tree is usually the more efficient choice. The two things to
respect: neural nets need more data than tabular trading problems often provide, and their flexibility means validation
has to be airtight or they'll fit noise. Used with enough data and disciplined validation, they open up problems the
simpler models can't reach.
LSTMs and sequence models. Built to model sequences, which is appealing because price and order flow are
sequential. The legitimate promise is capturing temporal dependencies that hand-built features might miss; the thing
to weigh is that you can often get most of that signal from well-engineered features (momentum over various
lookbacks, rolling statistics) fed to a simpler model, with far easier validation. Where the sequence structure is rich and
you have the data to support it, sequence models earn their place. Newer architectures (temporal convolutions,
attention/transformers) are also worth knowing and are covered in the dedicated module.
Reinforcement learning. Frames trading as an agent learning a policy through interaction, which is powerful for
sequential decision-making under uncertainty, position sizing and execution as much as entry signals. The realities to
plan for: RL is data-hungry, sensitive to how the environment and reward are designed, and exposed to the sim-to-real
gap, where a policy that excels in backtest behaves differently live. It demands the most care of the three, and with that
care it can address problems the others don't naturally express. The dedicated module covers environment design,
reward shaping, and the validation specifics.
The practical framing: complexity adds capability and adds degrees of freedom at the same time, so each step up the
ladder raises both the potential payoff and the validation burden. Start with the simplest model that could express your
AI Pathways · 2026 Page 5
hypothesis, and move to the advanced families when the problem calls for them and you have the data and discipline
to validate them. The advanced module is there for when you do.
PART 4 — VALIDATION: THE WAYS ML LIES, AND HOW TO
CATCH IT
This is the most important section in the module. It's longer and harder than the standard backtesting material because
ML introduces failure modes that simple strategies don't have. If you skip or skim this, you will eventually deploy an
overfit model and lose money, with near certainty. Read it twice.
The governing principle: assume every result is a false positive and design tests to prove it. A backtest is a hypothesis,
not a result.
12. Feature leakage (the silent killer)
Leakage is when information from the future, or information that wouldn't have existed at decision time, sneaks into
your model. It produces strong, entirely fake results, and it's easy to do by accident. It is the most common cause of a
backtest that looks good but fails live.
Common forms: - Look-ahead in features. Computing a feature for an open-of-day decision using that day's close or
high or low. The classic. - Normalization leakage. Scaling or z-scoring using the mean and standard deviation of the
entire dataset, including future data. Now your "past" features secretly know the future distribution. Scaling
parameters must be fit on training data only and applied forward. - Label leakage. Building the target with information
not yet available, or with overlapping windows that let adjacent samples share future information. - Survivorship and
selection. Training on the set of stocks that exist today, which silently excludes everything that went to zero, so the
model learns from a universe that only contains survivors.
The defense is relentless suspicion plus specific tests. Any time a result looks too good, suspect leakage first, before you
believe you found an edge. The build library includes a dedicated leakage-audit prompt that has Claude Code trace your
pipeline for every one of these.
13. The multiple-comparisons problem (the edge-discovery killer)
This is the failure mode that makes edge discovery specifically so dangerous, and most beginners have never heard of it.
If you test one feature, a good result is meaningful. If you test 500 features and 1,000 combinations and pick the best
ones, you are almost guaranteed to find some that score well purely by chance. Test enough random noise and some of
it will, by luck alone, produce a strong backtest. Standard backtesting cannot tell the difference between a real edge
and the luckiest of a thousand coin-flips, because it evaluates the winner in isolation as if you hadn't tried 999 others.
This is why naive edge discovery, searching a huge space and trading whatever backtested best, produces false positives
by construction. The more you search, the better your best fluke looks.
The defenses: - Track how many things you tested. The number of trials is essential context. A Sharpe of 2 from testing
one hypothesis is very different from a Sharpe of 2 selected as the best of 1,000. - Correct for it. Methods like the
deflated Sharpe ratio (below) explicitly adjust your performance estimate downward based on how many trials you ran
AI Pathways · 2026 Page 6
and how much you data-mined. - Demand robustness, not just a peak. A real edge tends to be surrounded by other
parameter settings that also work reasonably. A fluke is an isolated spike, brilliant at exactly one setting and garbage
one step away. If your best result is a lonely peak, distrust it. - Hold out data you never touch during search. A final
out-of-sample set the search never saw is your last line of defense against having fooled yourself across many trials.
14. Proper cross-validation for time series
Standard k-fold cross-validation, the default in most ML tutorials, is wrong for trading and will leak. It randomly shuffles
data into folds, which means you end up training on future data and testing on past data, and adjacent-in-time samples
(which share information) land in different folds. The result is a validation score that's far too optimistic.
Time-series data needs methods that respect the arrow of time:
Walk-forward (expanding or rolling window). Train on a past window, test on the next chunk forward, then roll or
expand and repeat. This mimics how you'd actually use the model: you only ever know the past. It's the baseline
method for time-series validation, and you should think in walk-forward terms by default.
Purged cross-validation. When your labels span multiple bars (as triple-barrier labels do), samples near the train/test
boundary can share information through their overlapping windows, leaking across the split. Purging removes training
samples whose label windows overlap the test set. It surgically cuts the contamination that even careful time-series
splits leave behind.
Embargo. On top of purging, you also drop a small buffer of training samples immediately after the test set, because
serial correlation can leak information just past the boundary. The embargo is that buffer. Purge plus embargo together
is the standard for CV on overlapping-label financial data.
Combinatorial purged cross-validation (CPCV). The advanced method. Instead of one train/test path, CPCV
systematically creates many different train/test combinations from purged, embargoed blocks, producing a distribution
of out-of-sample performance rather than a single number. This is powerful for two reasons: it uses your scarce data
efficiently, and it tells you not just "what was the backtest result" but "how much does the result vary depending on
which periods I tested," which is exactly the robustness question that exposes flukes. A single backtest path can be
lucky; a tight, healthy distribution across many CPCV paths is much harder to fake. This is the gold standard for serious
retail ML validation, and the build library walks Claude Code through implementing it.
15. Deflated and probabilistic Sharpe
A raw backtest Sharpe ratio is almost always inflated, for two reasons: you probably selected this strategy as the best of
many (multiple comparisons), and financial returns aren't normally distributed (fat tails and skew make the naive
Sharpe overstate quality).
Probabilistic Sharpe (PSR) asks: given the length of my track record and the non-normality of my returns, what's the
probability my true Sharpe is actually above some benchmark (often zero)? It converts a point estimate into a
confidence statement, which is more informative.
Deflated Sharpe (DSR) goes further and adjusts for the multiple-comparisons problem explicitly: it haircuts your Sharpe
based on how many strategy variants you tried before landing on this one. If you tested 1,000 configurations, DSR
knows your best one is probably partly luck and deflates accordingly. A strategy with a strong raw Sharpe can have a
deflated Sharpe near zero once you account for how much you searched, and that's the number that tells the truth.
AI Pathways · 2026 Page 7
The practical takeaway: never trust a raw backtest Sharpe from an ML search. Compute the deflated version with a full
count of how many things you tried. It is routine for a "Sharpe 2.5" discovery to deflate to "probably no real edge" once
the search is accounted for, and finding that out before you trade is the whole point.
16. What a real ML edge actually looks like
Calibrate your expectations so you're not chasing a fantasy:
Small. Real edges are modest. A genuine, sustainable edge might be a slight tilt in win rate or a modest Sharpe after
costs, not a 90% win rate or a smooth, always-up curve. Anything that looks extraordinary is overfit until proven
otherwise, and it usually can't be proven otherwise.
Noisy. A real edge has losing streaks, drawdowns, and stretches where it looks broken. The suspiciously smooth
equity curve is the red flag, not the goal. Real edges are bumpy because markets are noisy.
Decaying. Edges erode as markets adapt and as the conditions that created them change. A real edge has a shelf
life, sometimes months, and you should expect to monitor and eventually retire it.
Costly to capture. A signal with a tiny gross edge can be net-negative after spreads, slippage, and commissions.
Always validate net of realistic costs, not gross.
If your discovery is large, smooth, and looks permanent, the overwhelmingly likely explanation is that you fooled
yourself, not that you found the holy grail. The grail-shaped results are almost always artifacts.
PART 5 — DEPLOYMENT, DECAY, AND THE FULL WORKFLOW
17. An edge is not static
The work isn't done when validation passes. A discovered edge erodes over time as the market adapts, as other
participants find the same pattern, and as the regime that produced it shifts. Treating a validated model as permanent
is its own failure mode. Deployment is a monitoring problem, not a fire-and-forget one.
Monitoring for decay. Track the model's live performance against its validated expectations. Watch for the signal's
predictive power weakening (the gap between predicted and realized outcomes widening), the feature distributions
drifting away from what the model trained on (distribution shift), and the regime moving into territory the model
handles poorly. When live results fall persistently below the bottom of your validated performance distribution, the
edge may be gone.
Retraining discipline. You'll need to retrain periodically as new data arrives, but retraining is itself a trap if done
carelessly: retrain too often and you chase noise; retrain on a fixed schedule without checking and you may bake in a
dead edge. The discipline is to retrain on a sensible cadence, re-validate with the same rigor you used originally (purged
CV, deflated Sharpe, the works), and be willing to pull the model entirely if it no longer survives validation. A retrained
model is a new hypothesis, not an automatic continuation.
Paper-trade the live pipeline first. Even after a model validates beautifully on historical data, run the entire live pipeline
(data feed, feature computation, model inference, signal generation) in paper for a meaningful period before real
money. This catches the gap between your clean backtest and messy live reality: data feed quirks, computation timing,

latency, the things that don't show up in historical simulation. This is the same gate as the rest of Track B, and ML
doesn't get to skip it.
18. Most discovered edges die, and that's normal
A hard truth to set expectations: most edges you discover will not survive contact with rigorous validation, and of the
ones that do, many will not survive contact with live trading. This is not failure. This is the process working. The entire
point of the validation in Part 4 is to kill the false edges before they cost you money, which means a high kill rate is
exactly what success looks like.
A practitioner who discovers ten promising patterns, runs them through the process, and finds that eight were flukes,
one decayed in paper, and one is a small real edge worth trading, has done the job perfectly. The beginner who finds
ten patterns and trades all ten because they all backtested well is the one who blows up. Expect to kill most of your
ideas. Celebrate the killing, it's cheaper than the alternative.
19. The full workflow, start to finish
Putting it all together, the disciplined edge-discovery loop:
Hypothesize. Start with an economic or behavioral reason a pattern might exist, not a blind search. A hypothesis
with a "why" behind it is far less likely to be a fluke than one mined from pure data dredging.
Engineer features. Build clean, stationary, normalized features that could plausibly carry the signal. Audit for
leakage as you go.
Label properly. Use triple-barrier or another label that reflects the real trade and its risk management, not a naive
fixed-horizon "did it go up."
Baseline. Fit a regularized linear model first. If it finds nothing, be very skeptical of anything fancier.
Model. Train a gradient-boosted tree, conditioned on regime. Keep it as simple as the problem allows.
Validate hard. Purged, embargoed cross-validation; ideally combinatorial purged CV for a performance distribution.
Compute the deflated Sharpe with a full count of how many things you tried. Test net of realistic costs.
Audit for leakage and overfitting. Specifically hunt for the failure modes in Part 4 before believing any result.
Hold-out test. Evaluate once on data the entire search never touched. This is your last check before paper.
Paper-trade the live pipeline. Run the whole thing forward in paper before real money.
Deploy small, monitor for decay, retrain with discipline, and be willing to kill it.

PART 1 — DEEP LEARNING (NEURAL NETWORKS)
1. What a neural network actually is
A neural network is a stack of layers, each applying a linear transformation followed by a non-linear function, that
learns to map inputs to outputs by adjusting its internal weights to minimize a loss. With enough layers and units, it can
approximate very complex relationships, which is why it dominates problems like image recognition and language. The
power comes from learning its own representations of the data rather than relying on features you hand-craft.
For trading, that representational power is the appeal and the catch. It can discover structure you didn't think to
engineer as a feature. It can also discover structure that isn't really there, fitting the noise in a small, low signal-to-noise
dataset with great enthusiasm. The whole craft of deep learning in trading is getting the benefit without the cost, and
that is mostly a matter of data scale and validation discipline.
2. Where deep learning fits in trading
Neural nets earn their keep when the problem has the characteristics they're built for:
Rich or high-dimensional inputs. Order-book depth across many levels, cross-sectional panels of many assets and
features at once, or alternative data like text and images. When the input is large and structured, a network can find
patterns a tree on a few indicators cannot.
Alternative data. News, filings, social text, satellite or transaction data. Turning unstructured data into signal is a
natural neural-net job (often via embeddings), and it's an area where the edge is less picked over precisely because
it's harder.
Large datasets. Cross-sectional problems (modeling thousands of stocks over years) provide far more training
examples than a single instrument's time series, which is friendlier to deep models.

Where a network is usually not the efficient choice: a handful of tabular technical indicators on a single instrument.
That's a gradient-boosted tree's home turf, and a tree will typically match or beat a net there with far less data and far
easier validation. Reaching for a network there isn't wrong, it's just inefficient. Use the tool the problem calls for.
3. The architectures worth knowing
Feedforward (MLP). The basic fully-connected network. A reasonable starting point for tabular or panel data when
you want a neural baseline. Often a tree still wins on pure tabular, but an MLP is the neural baseline to compare
against before reaching for something fancier.
Convolutional (CNN). Built for grid-like data (images), but 1D convolutions are useful on sequences too, learning
local patterns over time windows efficiently. Temporal CNNs are a strong, often-underrated alternative to recurrent
models for time series.
Embeddings. A learned dense representation of categorical or high-cardinality inputs (a stock, a sector, a word).
Embeddings are how you feed things like "which ticker" or text into a network usefully, and they're a core technique
for alternative-data work.
Sequence-specific architectures (RNNs, LSTMs, attention/transformers) get their own part below, because the
sequential nature of markets makes them a distinct topic.
4. The discipline deep learning demands
The flexibility that makes neural nets powerful is exactly what you must constrain. The non-negotiables:
Enough data. Deep models are data-hungry. If you have a few thousand daily bars on one instrument, you're in a
regime where overfitting is the default outcome. Cross-sectional data, higher-frequency data, or alternative data
changes that. Assess whether you actually have the data the model needs.
Regularization, heavily. Dropout, weight decay, early stopping on a time-ordered validation set, and keeping the
architecture no larger than the problem needs. A smaller network that you can validate beats a larger one that
memorizes.
Airtight validation. Everything from the edge-discovery module: purged, embargoed, time-aware splits, never
random shuffling; net-of-cost evaluation; deflated performance accounting for how many architectures and
hyperparameters you tried. Neural nets have many more knobs, which means a worse multiple-comparisons
problem. Track your trials and count them all.
Interpretability effort. Networks are harder to interpret than trees, so you have to work for it: attribution methods,
probing what the model attends to, sanity-checking that important inputs make economic sense. If you can't form
any picture of why it works, you can't tell learning from memorization, and you should trust it less.
Deep learning is not off-limits for retail; it's a tool with a higher cost of entry. Pay the cost (data, regularization,
validation, interpretability) and it opens problems the simpler models can't touch. Skip the cost and it will hand you an
overfit model that looks good in backtest and fails live, the same as any flexible model would.

PART 2 — SEQUENCE MODELS (LSTMS AND BEYOND)
5. Why sequences are their own topic
Markets are sequential: today depends on yesterday, order flow arrives in time order, and the path matters. Sequence
models are architectures built specifically to model that temporal dependency, learning how past states inform future
ones rather than treating each observation as independent. The appeal in trading is direct: capture the temporal
structure that a static feature snapshot might miss.
The thing to weigh against that appeal: much of the temporal structure you care about can be captured by handengineered features (momentum over multiple lookbacks, rolling volatility, time-since-event) fed to a simpler model,
with far easier validation. So the question for any sequence-model project is whether the temporal dependencies are
rich enough, and your data deep enough, that letting the model learn them beats engineering them yourself. When the
answer is yes, sequence models are the right tool. When it's no, you've taken on a hard-to-validate model for signal you
could have gotten more cheaply.
6. The recurrent family
RNN (vanilla recurrent network). Processes a sequence step by step, carrying a hidden state forward. Conceptually
the foundation, but in practice it struggles to learn long-range dependencies (the vanishing-gradient problem), so
it's rarely used directly for serious work. Worth understanding as the basis for what follows.
LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory). The workhorse recurrent architecture. It adds gates that let the network learn
what to remember and what to forget over long sequences, solving much of the vanilla RNN's long-range problem.
For sequential trading problems where you want the model to learn temporal patterns, the LSTM is the classic
starting point.
GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit). A streamlined LSTM with fewer parameters. Often performs comparably with less data
and faster training, which makes it a sensible default to try alongside or before an LSTM, especially when data is
limited.
The recurrent family's strength is modeling order and memory. Its costs are real and worth planning for: recurrent
models can overfit on noisy financial data, they're sensitive to how you window and scale the inputs (a prime place for
leakage if you're not careful), and validating them takes the same purged, time-aware discipline as everything else,
applied to sequences rather than rows.
7. The modern alternatives
The field has moved beyond pure recurrence, and these are worth knowing because they often match or beat LSTMs:
Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN). 1D convolutions over the time axis. They capture local and (with dilation)
long-range patterns, train faster than recurrent models because they parallelize, and are often a strong, stable
alternative to LSTMs for time series. Frequently underrated; worth trying whenever you'd consider an LSTM.
Attention and Transformers. The architecture behind modern language models. Attention lets the model weigh
which past timesteps matter most for the current prediction, capturing long-range dependencies without

recurrence. Transformers are powerful and increasingly applied to financial sequences, with the caveat that they're
especially data-hungry, so they shine more on large cross-sectional or high-frequency datasets than on a single

The practical sequencing for a sequence-model project: try the simplest thing that could work (often well-engineered
features plus a simple model) as a baseline, then a GRU or LSTM, then a TCN, and reach for transformers when you
have the data scale to justify them. Always against the same baseline, always with the same validation rigor.
8. The leakage trap specific to sequences
Sequence models introduce leakage risks beyond the tabular ones, and they're easy to miss:
Windowing leakage. When you slice a series into input windows and target labels, overlapping windows across a
train/test boundary can share information. This is exactly what purging and embargoing exist to handle, applied to
the sequence windows.
Scaling leakage. Normalizing the sequence using statistics computed over the whole series (including the future) is
a common and easy-to-miss error. Scaling parameters must come from the training portion only.
Target alignment. It's easy to accidentally align the target so the model can see part of the answer in its input
window. Verify, explicitly, that every input timestep precedes the target it's predicting.
A sequence model that posts strong results is, like any flexible model, leaking until proven otherwise. The build library
includes a sequence-specific leakage audit for exactly this.
PART 3 — REINFORCEMENT LEARNING
9. What RL is, and why it's different
Supervised models (everything above) learn from labeled examples: here's an input, here's the right answer, learn the
mapping. Reinforcement learning is different in kind. An RL agent learns by interacting with an environment, taking
actions, receiving rewards, and adjusting its policy to maximize cumulative reward over time. There's no labeled "right
answer" for each step; the agent discovers what works through consequences.
For trading, this framing is powerful for one reason: trading is a sequential decision problem, not a one-shot prediction.
When to enter, how much to size, when to scale in or out, when to exit, these are interdependent decisions over time,
and RL is built to optimize sequences of decisions rather than individual predictions. That makes RL a natural fit for
execution, position sizing, and dynamic strategy management, areas where supervised models fit awkwardly.
10. The core concepts
Agent, environment, state, action, reward. The agent (your strategy) observes a state (market conditions, current
position), takes an action (buy, sell, hold, size), and receives a reward (profit, risk-adjusted return, or a shaped
signal). It repeats this over many episodes, learning a policy that maps states to good actions.
Policy. The agent's strategy: given this state, what action? Learning a good policy is the goal.

Reward design. The single most important and most dangerous choice in RL. The agent optimizes exactly what you
reward, including unintended loopholes. Reward raw profit and it may learn to take catastrophic risk for a good
backtest. Reward shape matters enormously: most serious trading RL rewards risk-adjusted returns or penalizes
drawdown explicitly, so the agent learns behavior you actually want.
Environment. A simulation of the market the agent trains in. Building a realistic environment (with costs, slippage,
and constraints) is most of the work, and the gap between your environment and reality is where RL strategies most
often break.
11. The realities to plan for
RL demands the most care of the three families, and the reasons are specific and manageable if you plan for them:
Data and sample efficiency. RL typically needs enormous amounts of interaction to learn. With limited historical
data, agents overfit the specific path of history they trained on. Techniques like training across many simulated
scenarios, randomizing the environment, and careful regularization help, but the data appetite is real.
Reward hacking. The agent will exploit any flaw in your reward or environment to maximize reward without
achieving what you actually wanted. A policy that looks brilliant may simply have found a loophole in your
simulator. Constant scrutiny of how the agent earns its reward, not just how much, is essential.
The sim-to-real gap. A policy trained in simulation faces a different live market: real slippage, real latency, real
regime shifts the simulator didn't contain. This gap is severe in RL because the agent's whole competence was
learned inside the simulation. Conservative environments (with realistic or pessimistic costs) and heavy out-ofsample and paper testing are how you close it.
Validation. Standard validation adapts awkwardly to RL because the agent's behavior changes the data it sees. You
lean on out-of-sample episodes, walk-forward retraining and testing, and especially long paper-trading of the live
policy. The discipline is the same goal as everywhere else (prove it works on data it didn't learn from), applied to an
agent rather than a predictor.
12. Where RL fits
RL is the most advanced and most demanding tool in the classroom, and it addresses problems the others don't
naturally express: dynamic sizing, execution, and managing a position through its life as a sequence of decisions.
Practitioners who value it are right that there's something there; the price of admission is realistic environment design,
careful reward shaping, scrutiny against reward hacking, and a clear-eyed view of the sim-to-real gap. Approached that
way, it's a legitimate and powerful part of an advanced toolkit. Approached carelessly, it produces a simulator
champion that loses money live. The build library walks Claude Code through environment design, reward shaping, and
the validation specifics so you're building it the careful way.

PART 4 — CHOOSING AND VALIDATING
13. Which family for which problem
A practical map:
Tabular signal on one or a few instruments → trees first (covered in the edge-discovery module). A neural net is
optional here and rarely the efficient choice.
Rich, high-dimensional, or alternative data → deep learning (MLP, CNN, embeddings). This is where networks pull
ahead.
Strong temporal dependencies, enough data → sequence models (GRU/LSTM, TCN, transformers), benchmarked
against an engineered-feature baseline.
Sequential decision-making: sizing, execution, position management → reinforcement learning, with a realistic
environment and shaped reward.
The meta-rule from the edge-discovery module still holds: start with the simplest model that could express your
hypothesis, prove it against a baseline, and step up to a more advanced family when the problem calls for it and you

14. The validation bar does not move
Whatever family you use, the validation discipline from the edge-discovery module applies in full and matters more,
because these models have more capacity to fit noise:
Time-aware, purged, embargoed splits. Never random shuffling.
Net-of-cost evaluation, always.
Deflated performance accounting for every architecture and hyperparameter you tried. More knobs means a worse
multiple-comparisons problem; count every trial.
A held-out set the search never touched.
Paper-trade the full live pipeline before real money, for longer with these models, since the sim-to-real and
overfitting risks are higher.
Monitor for decay and distribution shift, and be willing to retire a model that has stopped working.
The capability goes up with these families, and so does the validation burden. A strong result from a deep net, a
sequence model, or an RL agent is overfit or leaking until proven otherwise, exactly as it would be from any other
model, only more so. Bring the data, bring the discipline, and these tools earn their place.
