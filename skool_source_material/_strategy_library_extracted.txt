S T A R T  H E R E
How to use this libraryThis is a reference, not a course. Skim the family dividers to find a category that fits what you want to do,
then read the individual strategies. Each one is self-contained and ends with a Claude Code prompt that
builds it on your existing bot template, so you can go from reading about a strategy to backtesting it in one
paste.
These are starting templates, not plug-and-play systems
Every strategy here is a starting point you configure to your own plan, not a finished system to run as-is. The defaults
in each configuration table and build prompt are reasonable starting values, not recommendations for your account.
You set the position sizing, the stop and max loss per trade, and the strategy settings based on your capital, your risk
tolerance, and your own testing. Each build prompt is written to expose those as parameters and remind you to set
them. Backtest on your own data before any of this touches real money.
Four dimensions of fit: regime, time, day, and asset class
A strategy working is not just about the signal. Each entry tells you four things about where it fits. Regime is the best
and worst market state, usually tied to your HMM engine. Time of day covers whether it is a daily-close strategy or an
intraday one, and when in the session it works. Day and calendar covers any weekday or calendar-window
dependence. Asset class tells you which instruments it suits, since a rule built for index ETFs often fails on single small-
caps or commodities. Match all four to the strategy before you run it.
Standalone vs regime-gated
Most strategies here can run on their own, but nearly all of them work better when gated by your HMM regime
engine: mean reversion in calm regimes, trend in trending ones, premium selling switched off in crashes. Each entry
lists its best regime and the regime to avoid. The Regime & Allocation family at the end is the connective tissue that
decides how much of each strategy you run and when.
How each strategy is laid out
How it works and where the edge comes from explain the idea and the source of returns. When to use it covers
standalone vs regime-gated. The regime boxes, the time-day-asset fit, the timeframe, and the assets tell you where it
belongs. The configuration table lists the parameters and starting values. Risk and sizing is where you apply your own
rules. Watch out for and evidence cover the failure modes and how well documented the edge is, including how much
it has decayed since first published.

Ten families of strategyThe library is organized into ten families, ordered roughly from the most reliable and beginner-friendly to
the most advanced and dangerous. Mean reversion and trend come first because they are the workhorses.
Volatility, pairs, and the niche charting and microstructure families come later and demand more care. The
allocation family at the end wraps around everything.
1 Mean Reversion (8) Bet that price snaps back to an average after stretching too far.
2 Trend & Momentum (7) Bet that what is moving keeps moving.
3 Cross-Sectional / Rotation (5) Rank a basket of assets against each other and hold the leaders, rotate out of
laggards.
4 Volatility & Options (5) Trade volatility itself, or harvest the premium that option sellers collect on average.
5 Event & Calendar (6) Trade around scheduled events and calendar effects: earnings, index rebalances,
month-end, FOMC.
6 Pairs & Stat-Arb (4) Trade the spread between two related assets rather than direction.
7 Alternative Charting (4) Reframe price using bricks, ranges, or averaged candles to strip out noise.
8 Microstructure & Intraday (3) Short-horizon strategies that exploit the structure of the trading day: the opening
range, order-flow imbalance, gaps.
9 Seasonality & Macro (4) Calendar-season and macro-driven tilts: sell-in-May, the turn-of-year effect, sector
seasonality, carry.
10 Regime & Allocation (4) Not signals at all, but rules for how much to hold and when.

MR-01 RSI-2 Mean ReversionLarry Connors' classic: buy short-term oversold inside a long-term uptrend.
HOW IT WORKS
On a daily chart, only consider longs when price is above its 200-day moving average (the long-term trend is up). Buy when
the 2-period RSI drops below an oversold threshold (the short-term pullback). Exit when price closes back above a short
moving average (e.g. the 5-day) or RSI rises above a middle level. It is a pullback-buyer inside an uptrend, not a bottom-
picker in a downtrend.
WHERE THE EDGE COMES FROM
Short-term overreaction to selling inside an established uptrend. Buyers who panic-sell a dip in a rising market tend to be
wrong over a few days.
WHEN TO USE IT
Standalone on liquid index ETFs and large caps. Pairs extremely well with a regime filter: only take signals in a bull or low-vol
regime, skip entirely in crashes.
BEST REGIME
Bull, low-volatility, range-bound-up
AVOID IN
Crash, sustained bear (the 200-day filter handles most of
this, but a regime gate adds protection)

Size by percent of equity (2 percent risk per trade is a sane default). Because mean reversion has a high win rate but
occasional large losers, a hard stop matters more than usual: place it well below entry (e.g. 2x ATR or a fixed percent) so a

drop it.
EVIDENCE
Connors and Alvarez documented RSI-2 extensively on US equity indices with strong historical win rates. It is one of the most-
replicated short-term mean-reversion rules. Edge has compressed as it became well known, so treat modern expectations as
more modest than the original backtests.

MR-02 Bollinger Band ReversionFade moves that pierce the outer band back toward the middle band.
HOW IT WORKS
Plot a moving average (the middle band, e.g. 20-day) with bands at plus/minus 2 standard deviations. Buy when price closes
below the lower band (stretched cheap), exit when it returns to the middle band. Optionally short when it closes above the
upper band in a range-bound market. The bands adapt to volatility, so signals self-scale as the market gets noisier or calmer.
WHERE THE EDGE COMES FROM
Statistical overextension. In a range-bound market, price spends most of its time near the mean, so a 2-sigma stretch is more
likely to revert than to keep going.
WHEN TO USE IT
Best in range-bound, mean-reverting markets. As a standalone it whipsaws badly in trends, so gate it: only fade bands when
a trend filter says the market is sideways, or only take the long side in an uptrend.
BEST REGIME
Range-bound, low-volatility
AVOID IN
Strong trend (price 'walks the band' and you fade a freight
train), crash
WHEN TO RUN IT: TIME, DAY & ASSET FIT
TIME OF DAY

the shorter the bar.

Percent-of-equity sizing. Stop goes a defined distance beyond the band (e.g. 1 ATR below the lower band) because 'price
walking the band' in a trend is the main failure mode and you need to be out fast. If shorting the upper band, size smaller;
upside squeezes are violent.
WATCH OUT FOR
In a trend, price rides the outer band for a long time and naive fading produces a string of losses. The fix is a trend/range
filter. Also, bands widen in volatility, so a 2-sigma event in a crash is not a reversion signal, it is a falling knife.
EVIDENCE
Band-reversion is widely used and documented; its edge is real in range regimes and negative in trends, which is why regime
conditioning matters so much. Performance is regime-dependent, not universal.

MR-03 Z-Score ReversionTrade the standardized distance of price from its own moving average.
HOW IT WORKS
Compute a rolling mean and rolling standard deviation of price (or of the price/MA ratio). The z-score is (price minus mean)
divided by standard deviation. Enter long when z drops below a negative threshold (e.g. -2), exit when z returns toward 0.
This is the engine underneath most mean-reversion and pairs strategies, expressed directly. It generalizes cleanly to spreads
for pairs trading.
WHERE THE EDGE COMES FROM
Same overextension logic as Bollinger, but expressed as a continuous, tunable distance rather than a fixed band, which
makes conviction-scaling natural.
WHEN TO USE IT
Standalone on single names in range regimes, and as the core signal for pairs/spread trading. Conviction can scale with the
magnitude of z, which fits a sizing-by-conviction system well.
BEST REGIME
Range-bound, low-volatility, mean-reverting
AVOID IN
Trending, crash
WHEN TO RUN IT: TIME, DAY & ASSET FIT
TIME OF DAY
Flexible: daily for swing, intraday for
liquid instruments. The lookback should
match the bar (e.g. 20 daily bars vs 20
5-minute bars).
DAY / CALENDAR
Neutral.
ASSET CLASS
Single equities, ETFs, and spreads/pairs.
The same engine drives pairs trading,
where it extends to two-asset spreads
regardless of the underlying class.
TIMEFRAME
Daily or intraday
ASSETS
Single equities, ETFs, and (as a spread) pairs
CONFIGURATION
PARAMETER DEFAULT (SET YOUR OWN)NOTES
lookback 20 to 60 Window for the rolling mean and std. Shorter = more reactive, noisier.
entry_z -2.0 Enter long when z falls below this.
exit_z 0.0 to -0.5 Exit as z reverts toward the mean.
max_z_stop -3.5 If z keeps falling past this, the relationship may be broken; stop out.
scale_by_z true Optionally scale position size with |z| up to a cap.
RISK & SIZING — SET TO YOUR OWN PLAN
Percent-of-equity, optionally scaled by the z magnitude up to a hard cap so a -4 z does not produce an oversized position.
The max-z stop is the key control: a z that blows through your stop level usually means the mean has shifted, not that
reversion is imminent.

WATCH OUT FOR
Z-score assumes the mean is stable. In a regime shift the 'mean' moves and z stays extreme while you bleed. Short lookbacks
make z jumpy and overtrade. Do not treat an extreme z as automatically more attractive; past a point it signals a broken
relationship.
EVIDENCE
The standardized-distance approach is the textbook basis for statistical mean reversion and pairs trading. It works where the
series is genuinely stationary and fails where it is not, which is why a stationarity or regime check belongs alongside it.

MR-04 VW AP Reversion (Intraday)Fade intraday stretches away from the volume-weighted average price.
HOW IT WORKS
VWAP is the average price weighted by volume since the session open, and it acts as an intraday magnet because institutions
benchmark fills to it. When price stretches a configurable distance above or below VWAP (often measured in standard-
deviation bands around VWAP), fade back toward it. Exit on the touch of VWAP or a fixed target. Strictly an intraday, flat-by-
close strategy.
WHERE THE EDGE COMES FROM
Institutional execution anchors to VWAP , creating real flow that pulls price back toward it intraday, especially in range-bound
sessions.
WHEN TO USE IT
Intraday on liquid instruments. Best on range/balanced days; turn it off on strong trend days (gap-and-go, news) where price
leaves VWAP and never looks back.
BEST REGIME
Balanced/range intraday sessions
AVOID IN
Trend days, news-driven directional sessions

MR-05 Gap FadeFade opening gaps that have a statistical tendency to fill.
HOW IT WORKS
When a liquid name opens a configurable percent away from the prior close (a gap up or down) without major news, take
the opposite side expecting the gap to partially or fully fill during the session. Exit at the prior close (gap fill) or a partial
target, with a stop beyond the open. Works best on gaps that are emotional/overnight-driven rather than fundamental.
WHERE THE EDGE COMES FROM
Overnight overreaction. Many gaps are driven by thin overnight liquidity and sentiment, and reverse once full liquidity
returns at the open.
WHEN TO USE IT
Intraday, single names and index ETFs. Critical filter: skip gaps caused by earnings or hard news, which gap for a reason and
keep going (that is PEAD territory, the opposite trade).
BEST REGIME
Calm/range markets, non-news gaps
AVOID IN
Earnings/news gaps, crash mornings, strong trend regimes
WHEN TO RUN IT: TIME, DAY & ASSET FIT
TIME OF DAY

the first hour as the gap shows whether
it will fill; flatten by close. The edge
lives in the morning.

creates more real-news gaps to

on illiquid names are unpredictable; the

MR-06 Internal Bar Strength (IBS)Buy when the close sits near the low of the day's range; a simple, robust ETF reversion.
HOW IT WORKS
IBS = (close minus low) / (high minus low), a value from 0 to 1 measuring where the close landed within the bar's range. A
low IBS (close near the low) is short-term oversold. Buy when IBS is below a threshold (e.g. 0.2), exit the next day or when
IBS is high (e.g. above 0.8). Dead simple, one input, and historically robust on equity index ETFs.
WHERE THE EDGE COMES FROM
Short-term overreaction captured by where the close sits in the range. A close on the lows after a down day often bounces.
WHEN TO USE IT
Standalone on index ETFs, especially mean-reverting ones. Combines well with a regime filter and with RSI-2 (they agree
often but capture slightly different things).
BEST REGIME
Range-bound and bull ETFs, low-volatility
AVOID IN
Crash, strong downtrend
WHEN TO RUN IT: TIME, DAY & ASSET FIT
TIME OF DAY
Daily close signal; act next session. Not
intraday.
DAY / CALENDAR
Neutral.
ASSET CLASS
Equity index ETFs specifically. IBS is an
ETF effect; on single stocks the close-in-
range signal is drowned out by
idiosyncratic moves.
TIMEFRAME
Daily; holding 1 to 3 days
ASSETS
Equity index ETFs (SPY , QQQ, IWM, sector ETFs)
CONFIGURATION

ibs_entry 0.2 Buy when IBS is below this (close near the low).

Percent-of-equity sizing with a hard stop (e.g. 2 ATR). Same discipline as other mean reversion: the win rate is high, so the

AVOID IN

PR-01 Static-Pairs Z-ScoreTrade the spread between two historically related assets back to its mean.
HOW IT WORKS
Pick two assets that move together (e.g. two stocks in the same industry, or an ETF and a close peer). Build the spread (a
hedge-ratio-weighted difference of their prices), compute its z-score, and when the spread stretches (|z| > 2) go long the
cheap leg and short the rich leg, expecting convergence. Exit when the spread reverts (|z| < 0.5). Market-neutral: you profit
from the relationship, not market direction.

Mean reversion of an economically linked spread: two similar assets should not diverge permanently, so temporary
divergences tend to converge.

ETF vs its near-substitute). It is the classic stat-arb starter, but it MUST be paired with correlation-health monitoring because
the relationship can break.

AC-01 Renko TrendUse fixed-size price bricks to strip out noise and trade the cleaned trend.

RA-03 Risk-Parity AllocationSize positions so each contributes equal risk, not equal dollars.
HOW IT WORKS
Instead of equal-dollar or cap-weighted allocation, risk parity sizes each asset so its risk contribution to the portfolio is equal.
Low-volatility assets (bonds) get larger weights, high-volatility assets (stocks) smaller, often with modest leverage to reach a
target return. The goal is a more balanced, diversified risk exposure than a stock-heavy 60/40, which is dominated by equity
risk.

Diversification efficiency: equal-dollar portfolios concentrate risk in the most volatile asset, while equalizing risk
contributions produces better-diversified, more stable risk-adjusted returns across regimes.

RA-04 Fractional-Kelly Position SizingSize positions by a fraction of the Kelly criterion to balance growth against drawdown.
HOW IT WORKS
The Kelly criterion gives the bet size that maximizes long-run growth given a strategy's edge and odds. Full Kelly is too
aggressive in practice (estimation error and fat tails make it ruinously volatile), so practitioners use a fraction, commonly a
half or quarter Kelly. This is a sizing layer: estimate your strategy's edge and variance, compute the Kelly fraction, scale it
down by your chosen fraction, and cap it. It decides how much, not what.
WHERE THE EDGE COMES FROM
Not a signal but a growth-optimal sizing framework: Kelly maximizes long-run compounding for a known edge, and using a
fraction trades a little growth for a large reduction in volatility and drawdown.
WHEN TO USE IT
As a sizing layer on any strategy with an estimable edge and win/loss distribution. Best combined with volatility targeting
and your regime exposure dial. For members who want a principled alternative to fixed-percent sizing.
