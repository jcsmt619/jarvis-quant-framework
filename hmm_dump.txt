MM Overview

Overview: A fundamental flaw in retail systematic trading is the assumption of market stationarity—the idea that the market behaves the same way today as it did yesterday. In reality, financial markets constantly shift between distinct macroscopic states, known as regimes (e.g., low-volatility bull markets, high-volatility bear markets, or choppy sideways consolidation).

If a trend-following algorithm is left running during a sideways regime, it will experience severe drawdown through continuous whipsawing.

The Quantitative Approach: Instead of manually guessing the current market environment, quantitative systems use probabilistic models to mathematically infer the current regime. The industry standard for this is the Hidden Markov Model (HMM).

An HMM assumes that the underlying "state" of the market is hidden from us. However, we can observe the "emissions" of that state—specifically, the daily returns and the daily volatility. By feeding these observables into an HMM, the algorithm calculates the exact probability that the market has transitioned from one state to another, allowing the overarching trading system to dynamically adjust its capital allocation, alter its risk parameters, or switch strategies entirely.

In this module, we will construct an HMM pipeline to classify market regimes automatically. HMM Engine

This engine downloads historical data, calculates the necessary statistical observables, trains the machine learning model, and outputs a visual map of the market's hidden states.

We use hmmlearn, the industry-standard Python library for Markov models. The algorithm is instructed to look for 3 specific states, which typically align with:

Low Volatility / Positive Return (Bull)

High Volatility / Negative Return (Bear/Crash)

Moderate Volatility / Flat Return (Sideways/Chop)

The model uses the Viterbi Algorithm to decode the sequence of observations and assign a discrete state (0, 1, or 2) to every single trading day in the dataset, completely automatically.

Knowing the current market regime is only half the battle. To actually protect your capital, your trading system needs to know what to do with that information.

Professional quant funds don't force one single strategy to trade in every market condition. They know that a trend-following strategy will bleed money in a choppy, sideways market. They also know a dip-buying strategy will get completely crushed during a bear market crash.

This is where we use the HMM as a master switch. When the HMM detects that the market has shifted from a "Bull" regime to a "Chop" regime, it automatically turns off the trend-following strategy and turns on a mean-reversion strategy. It actively matches the right tool to the right environment.

Implementation: We are going to build three distinct trading strategies to match our three HMM states:

Trend Following (Bull Market): Buys when the market is pushing higher with low volatility.

Mean Reversion (Choppy Market): Buys the bottom of the range and sells the top of the range when the market is moving sideways.

Cash / Risk-Off (Bear Market): Halts all trading and sits in cash during high-volatility crashes.

By combining these strategies with the HMM's daily output, the Python script will automatically swap its core trading logic the moment the market environment changes.
